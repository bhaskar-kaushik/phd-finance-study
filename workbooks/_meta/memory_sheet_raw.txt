Part I - Banking, Liquidity, Monetary Policy, and Crises
W01-01 | 1/20/2026 | JF
Anil K. Kashyap, Raghuram Rajan, and Jeremy C. Stein (2002) - Banks as Liquidity Providers: An Explanation for
the Coexistence of Lending and Deposit-Taking
Main point: Banks can efficiently combine deposit-taking and lending because withdrawal shocks and credit-line drawdowns are
not perfectly correlated, so the same liquidity buffer can serve both sides of the balance sheet.
W01-02 | 1/20/2026 | JF
Evan Gatev and Philip E. Strahan (2006) - Banks' Advantage in Hedging Liquidity Risk: Theory and Evidence from
the Commercial Paper Market
Main point: When market liquidity dries up, firms draw on bank credit lines but deposits also tend to flow into banks, giving banks
a natural hedge and a comparative advantage in supplying liquidity.
W01-03 | 1/22/2026 | AER
Adam B. Ashcraft (2005) - Are Banks Really Special? New Evidence from the FDIC-Induced Failure of Healthy
Banks
Main point: The failure of even healthy banks hurts local economic activity, showing that banks matter not only because of
borrower quality but because relationship lending and credit supply have real effects.
W01-04 | 1/22/2026 | RFS
Allen N. Berger and Christa H. S. Bouwman (2009) - Bank Liquidity Creation
Main point: This paper builds a broad measure of liquidity creation and shows that banks create liquidity by funding illiquid
assets with liquid liabilities and off-balance-sheet commitments, with important links to bank capital and size.
W02-01 | 1/27/2026 | AER
Anil K. Kashyap and Jeremy C. Stein (2000) - What Do a Million Observations on Banks Say about the
Transmission of Monetary Policy?
Main point: Monetary tightening reduces lending more at smaller, less liquid banks, supporting the bank balance-sheet channel
of monetary policy.

W02-02 | 1/27/2026 | AER
Gabriel Jimenez, Steven Ongena, Jose-Luis Peydro, and Jesus Saurina (2012) - Credit Supply and Monetary
Policy: Identifying the Bank Balance-Sheet Channel with Loan Applications
Main point: Using Spanish loan applications, the paper cleanly separates credit supply from credit demand and shows that
tighter monetary policy cuts credit supply especially at weaker banks, with real effects on firms.
W02-03 | 1/29/2026 | QJE
Itamar Drechsler, Alexi Savov, and Philipp Schnabl (2017) - The Deposits Channel of Monetary Policy
Main point: Banks with market power do not fully pass through rate hikes to depositors; instead they widen deposit spreads, lose
deposits, and contract lending, creating a deposits channel of monetary policy.
W02-04 | 1/29/2026 | RFS
Alexander Rodnyansky and Olivier M. Darmouni (2017) - The Effects of Quantitative Easing on Bank Lending
Behavior
Main point: QE raised the value of banks' MBS holdings and effectively recapitalized exposed banks, which then expanded
lending more than less-exposed banks.
W03-01 | 2/3/2026 | QJE
Jith Jayaratne and Philip E. Strahan (1996) - The Finance-Growth Nexus: Evidence from Bank Branch
Deregulation
Main point: Branch deregulation increased state growth, mainly by improving the quality and efficiency of bank lending rather
than by simply raising the volume of credit.
W03-02 | 2/3/2026 | AER
Joe Peek and Eric S. Rosengren (2000) - Collateral Damage: Effects of the Japanese Bank Crisis on Real Activity
in the United States
Main point: A foreign banking shock transmitted to U.S. borrowers through Japanese bank affiliates, showing that bank distress
can spill across borders and depress real activity.
W03-03 | 2/5/2026 | AER
Asim Ijaz Khwaja and Atif Mian (2008) - Tracing the Impact of Bank Liquidity Shocks: Evidence from an Emerging
Market
Main point: By comparing the same firm borrowing from different banks, the paper isolates bank-supply shocks and shows that
a liquidity hit to a bank leads to a clear contraction in its lending.
W03-04 | 2/5/2026 | QJE
Mary Amiti and David E. Weinstein (2011) - Exports and Financial Shocks
Main point: Deterioration in the health of banks providing trade finance causes exports to fall relative to domestic sales, helping
explain why exports collapse so sharply in crises.
W04-01 | 2/10/2026 | JFE
Gary Gorton and Andrew Metrick (2012) - Securitized Banking and the Run on Repo
Main point: The 2007-2008 crisis looked like a modern bank run in the repo market: wholesale short-term funding of securitized
assets suddenly became unstable, triggering a systemic panic.

W04-02 | 2/10/2026 | QJE
Benjamin J. Keys, Tanmoy Mukherjee, Amit Seru, and Vikrant Vig (2010) - Did Securitization Lead to Lax
Screening? Evidence from Subprime Loans
Main point: Securitization weakened originators' screening incentives in subprime lending, and the effect was strongest where
loans were easiest to sell rather than keep.
W04-03 | 2/12/2026 | QJE
Atif Mian and Amir Sufi (2009) - The Consequences of Mortgage Credit Expansion: Evidence from the U.S.
Mortgage Default Crisis
Main point: Mortgage credit expanded most in poorer and more levered zip codes, fueling a housing boom followed by
especially severe defaults and household distress.
W04-04 | 2/12/2026 | JFE
Victoria Ivashina and David Scharfstein (2010) - Bank Lending During the Financial Crisis of 2008
Main point: Syndicated lending collapsed during the crisis, with weaker and nonbank lenders pulling back most and credit
concentrating more in stronger banks and existing relationships.
W05-01 | 2/17/2026 | AER
Rajkamal Iyer and Manju Puri (2012) - Understanding Bank Runs: The Importance of Depositor-Bank
Relationships and Networks
Main point: Depositor behavior in a run depends on information, relationships, and social networks: informed or connected
depositors run earlier and panic spreads through local ties.
W05-02 | 2/17/2026 | AER
Mark Egan, Ali Hortacsu, and Gregor Matvos (2017) - Deposit Competition and Financial Fragility: Evidence from
the US Banking Sector
Main point: Competition for uninsured deposits creates fragility because uninsured depositors discipline weak banks and can
run, giving the banking system multiple-equilibrium features.
W05-03 | 2/19/2026 | AER
Lawrence Schmidt, Allan Timmermann, and Russ Wermers (2016) - Runs on Money Market Mutual Funds
Main point: Prime money market funds are vulnerable to strategic runs because early redeemers are protected while remaining
investors absorb losses, recreating classic first-mover fragility.
W05-04 | 2/19/2026 | JFE
Erica Xuewei Jiang, Gregor Matvos, Tomasz Piskorski, and Amit Seru (2024) - Monetary Tightening and U.S. Bank
Fragility in 2023: Mark-to-Market Losses and Uninsured Depositor Runs?
Main point: The paper shows that large unrealized asset losses made many banks vulnerable in 2023, but actual fragility
depended crucially on uninsured deposit funding and the possibility of rapid depositor runs.
Part II - Corporate Risk Management, Hedging, and Incentives
W06-01 | 2/26/2026 | JF
Peter Tufano (1996) - Who Manages Risk? An Empirical Examination of Risk Management Practices in the Gold
Mining Industry
Main point: Hedging choices in gold mining are shaped not just by firm fundamentals but also by managerial incentives:
managers with more option-like pay hedge less, while those with more stock-based exposure hedge more.

W07-01 | 3/3/2026 | JF
Christopher C. Geczy, Bernadette A. Minton, and Catherine M. Schrand (1997) - Why Firms Use Currency
Derivatives
Main point: Firms are more likely to use currency derivatives when they face meaningful foreign exchange exposure and when
financial frictions make cash-flow smoothing especially valuable.
W07-02 | 3/3/2026 | JF
John R. Graham and Daniel A. Rogers (2002) - Do Firms Hedge in Response to Tax Incentives?
Main point: Hedging does not seem driven much by convex tax schedules, but it increases debt capacity and interest tax
shields, linking risk management to capital structure.
W07-03 | 3/5/2026 | JF
David Haushalter (2000) - Financing Policy, Basis Risk, and Corporate Hedging: Evidence from Oil and Gas
Producers
Main point: Oil and gas firms hedge more when financial distress and underinvestment concerns are greater, but their choices
are constrained by basis risk between their true exposures and available contracts.
W07-04 | 3/5/2026 | RFS
George Allayannis and James P. Weston (2001) - The Use of Foreign Currency Derivatives and Firm Market Value
Main point: Firms using currency derivatives trade at higher values on average, consistent with hedging reducing costly
exposure and supporting investment.
W08-01 | 3/10/2026 | JF
Yanbo Jin and Philippe Jorion (2006) - Firm Value and Hedging: Evidence from U.S. Oil and Gas Producers
Main point: For oil and gas producers, hedging has little average effect on firm value, suggesting that the benefits of risk
management are not automatically large or easy to detect in market values.
W08-02 | 3/10/2026 | RFS
Erik P. Gilje and Jerome P. Taillard (2017) - Does Hedging Affect Firm Value? Evidence from a Natural Experiment
Main point: A shock that impaired hedging ability shows that hedging supports value and real activity by easing financing
frictions and protecting investment when cash flows become riskier.
W08-03 | 3/12/2026 | JFE
Viral V. Acharya, Lars A. Lochstoer, and Tarun Ramadorai (2013) - Limits to Arbitrage and Hedging: Evidence
from Commodity Markets
Main point: Commodity futures prices reflect producers' hedging demand when intermediary capital is limited; arbitrageurs
cannot fully absorb hedging pressure, so risk premia emerge endogenously.
W08-04 | 3/12/2026 | RFS
Michael Dewally, Louis H. Ederington, and Chitru S. Fernando (2013) - Determinants of Trader Profits in
Commodity Futures Markets
Main point: Trader profits in commodity futures are systematic rather than random: they differ across trader types because
information, hedging needs, and market structure shape who earns rents.

W09-01 | 3/24/2026 | JAE
Todd A. Gormley, David A. Matsa, and Todd Milbourn (2013) - CEO Compensation and Corporate Risk: Evidence
from a Natural Experiment
Main point: A compensation shock that changes executives' risk-taking incentives leads firms to alter risk, showing that
managerial pay design causally affects corporate risk choices.
W09-02 | 3/24/2026 | JFE
Tor-Erik Bakke, Hamed Mahmudi, Chitru S. Fernando, and Jesus M. Salas (2016) - The Causal Effect of Option
Pay on Corporate Risk Management
Main point: More convex option-based pay makes managers tolerate more firm risk and hedge less, providing causal evidence
that executive compensation shapes corporate risk management.
W09-03 | 3/26/2026 | JB
Gregory W. Brown, Peter R. Crabb, and David Haushalter (2006) - Are Firms Successful at Selective Hedging?
Main point: Firms do try to time markets with selective hedging, but on average they do not appear to generate economically
meaningful gains from doing so.
W09-04 | 3/26/2026 | JF
Christopher C. Geczy, Bernadette A. Minton, and Catherine M. Schrand (2007) - Taking a View: Corporate
Speculation, Governance, and Compensation
Main point: Some firms use derivatives to speculate rather than hedge, and this behavior is related to governance and CFO
incentives, especially when firms believe taking a view can be profitable.
W10-01 | 3/31/2026 | JFE
Tim Adam (2009) - Capital Expenditures, Financial Constraints, and the Use of Options
Main point: Financially constrained firms are more likely to use nonlinear option strategies because options protect downside
investment capacity while preserving upside exposure.
W10-02 | 3/31/2026 | JFE
Gerard Hoberg and S. Katie Moon (2017) - Offshore Activities and Financial vs Operational Hedging
Main point: Firms can substitute operational hedges for financial hedges: offshore production and geographic flexibility reduce
the need for derivative-based risk management.
W10-03 | 4/2/2026 | JF
Thomas W. Bates, Kathleen M. Kahle, and Rene M. Stulz (2009) - Why Do U.S. Firms Hold So Much More Cash
than They Used To?
Main point: The long-run rise in corporate cash mainly reflects a shift toward riskier, R&D-intensive firms with weaker internal
cash flows, not simply managerial waste.
W10-04 | 4/2/2026 | RFS
Rudiger Fahlenbrach, Kevin Rageth, and Rene M. Stulz (2021) - How Valuable Is Financial Flexibility when
Revenue Stops? Evidence from the COVID-19 Crisis
Main point: During COVID, firms that entered the shock with more cash, less short-term debt, and stronger flexibility performed
better, showing the value of balance-sheet slack in extreme downturns.

Part III - ESG, Climate, Reputation, and Nonfinancial Risk
W11-01 | 4/7/2026 | SMJ
Mark P. Sharfman and Chitru S. Fernando (2008) - Environmental Risk Management and the Cost of Capital
Main point: Better environmental risk management lowers firms' cost of capital by reducing both operating risk and financing
frictions.
W11-02 | 4/7/2026 | JFE
Harrison Hong and Marcin Kacperczyk (2009) - The Price of Sin: The Effects of Social Norms on Markets
Main point: Sin stocks are shunned by norm-constrained investors and institutions, receive less coverage, and therefore earn
higher expected returns as compensation for neglect.
W11-03 | 4/9/2026 | MS
Sudheer Chava (2014) - Environmental Externalities and Cost of Capital
Main point: Environmentally harmful firms face a higher cost of capital and tighter financing, especially in debt markets, because
investors and lenders price environmental externalities.
W11-04 | 4/9/2026 | JFQA
Chitru S. Fernando, Mark P. Sharfman, and Vahap B. Uysal (2017) - Corporate Environmental Policy and
Shareholder Value: Following the Smart Money
Main point: The market tends to reward stronger environmental policy when sophisticated investors move first, suggesting that
at least part of environmental policy is value-relevant rather than purely cosmetic.
W12-01 | 4/14/2026 | JF
Karl V. Lins, Henri Servaes, and Ane Tamayo (2017) - Social Capital, Trust, and Firm Performance: The Value of
Corporate Social Responsibility during the Financial Crisis
Main point: CSR acted like social capital during the financial crisis: high-CSR firms earned more trust, suffered less, and
performed better when confidence in corporations collapsed.
W12-02 | 4/14/2026 | JFE
Brandon N. Cline, Ralph A. Walkling, and Adam S. Yore (2018) - The Consequences of Managerial Indiscretions:
Sex, Lies, and Firm Value
Main point: Personal misconduct by executives destroys firm value because markets infer weaker governance, poorer
judgment, and reputational damage from private indiscretions.
W12-03 | 4/16/2026 | JFE
Asaf Bernstein, Matthew T. Gustafson, and Ryan Lewis (2019) - Disaster on the Horizon: The Price Effect of Sea
Level Rise
Main point: Real estate markets increasingly discount properties exposed to sea level rise, especially where investors are more
sophisticated and climate risk is more salient.
W12-04 | 4/16/2026 | JFE
Asaf Bernstein, Stephen B. Billings, Matthew T. Gustafson, and Ryan Lewis (2022) - Partisan Residential Sorting
on Climate Change Risk
Main point: Beliefs matter for climate pricing: over time, climate-exposed properties are increasingly owned by people with
different political views, showing that sorting helps shape who bears climate risk.

W13-01 | 4/21/2026 | RFS
Justin Murfin, Matthew Spiegel, and Jose A. Scheinkman (2020) - Is the Risk of Sea Level Rise Capitalized in
Residential Real Estate?
Main point: Using variation in local land subsidence, the paper finds limited average capitalization of sea-level-rise risk in home
prices, emphasizing that climate pricing can be incomplete.
W13-02 | 4/21/2026 | RFS
Jawad M. Addoum, David T. Ng, Ariel Ortiz-Bobea, and Harrison Hong (2020) - Temperature Shocks and
Establishment Sales
Main point: Temporary temperature shocks reduce establishment sales and productivity, giving a direct firm-level channel
through which climate exposure affects cash flows.
W13-03 | 4/23/2026 | RFS
Philipp Krueger, Zacharias Sautner, and Laura T. Starks (2020) - The Importance of Climate Risks for Institutional
Investors
Main point: Large investors view climate risk as financially material, especially over longer horizons, but they differ sharply in
whether they see it as primarily a risk-management problem or a return opportunity.
W13-04 | 4/23/2026 | JFE
Alexander Dyck, Karl V. Lins, Lukas Roth, and Hannes F. Wagner (2019) - Do Institutional Investors Drive
Corporate Social Responsibility? International Evidence
Main point: Institutional investors appear to causally improve firms' environmental and social performance, implying that
ownership can shape corporate behavior rather than merely select into it.
W14-01 | 4/28/2026 | RFS
Qing Li, Hongyu Shan, Yuehua Tang, and Vincent Yao (2024) - Corporate Climate Risk: Measurements and
Responses
Main point: Using earnings-call text, the paper builds firm-level measures of physical and transition climate risk and shows that
exposed firms change disclosure, investment, and adaptation behavior.
W14-02 | 4/28/2026 | JF
Ran Duchin, Janet Gao, and Qiping Xu (2025) - Sustainability or Greenwashing: Evidence from the Asset Market
for Industrial Pollution
Main point: When pollutive assets are sold, pollution often shifts to private owners rather than disappearing, so some
'sustainable' asset sales look more like greenwashing than genuine environmental improvement.
W14-03 | 4/30/2026 | WP
Benjamin J. Keys and Philip Mulder (working paper, revised 2025) - Property Insurance and Disaster Risk: New
Evidence from Mortgage Escrow Data
Main point: Using inferred insurance premiums from mortgage escrows, the paper shows homeowners insurance costs have
risen rapidly and increasingly reflect local disaster and climate risk.
W14-04 | 4/30/2026 | WP
Olimpia Carradori, Felix von Meyerinck, and Zacharias Sautner (working paper, revised 2026) - Insurers' Carbon
Underwriting Policies
Main point: Large insurers are adopting carbon underwriting policies, but implementation is incomplete and heterogeneous, so
climate commitments do not automatically translate into reduced real-economy fossil exposure.

Shinichi Kamiya, Jun-Koo Kang, Jungmin Kim, Andreas Milidonis, and Rene M. Stulz (2021) - Risk Management,
Firm Reputation, and the Impact of Successful Cyberattacks on Target Firms

Main point: Unexpected data breaches destroy brand and equity value, increase customer churn, and induce firms to repair
